Ho`okāhi no lā o ka malihini.

This site is a collection of Politics of Place [POP] Tours designed by students in an undergraduate class on Hawai`i Politics at the University of Hawai`i at Mānoa (Spring 2016). POP Tours are essentially walking tours that address the politics of a particular place that are held within the land and the people that live there. The POP Tours shared here focus on Hawai`i.

The students used their POP Tours to address topics of their choice. Here, it is possible to take POP Tours on Food Security, Sports Tourism, Luxury Development in Kaka`ako, Plantation history on the Big Island, the Overthrow of the Kingdom of Hawai`i in 1893, plus many more!

POP Tours help us explore our relationship to place. They were created in a course that was premised on the idea that depending on who we are and where we are, your relationship to place is going to change. In Hawai`i, for example, whether we are visitors, guests, foreigners, exchange students, arrivants, descendants of families that have been in Hawai`i for multiple generations, Kānaka `Oiwi, none of these or all of these; we all have one thing in common: we are living in a placeHawai`iwhere the original Peoples and lands are currently and forcibly occupied by a foreign nationThe United States of America. There is an active Aloha `Āina independence movement to regain ea (sovereignty or breath) in the Archipelago. As students in Hawai`i, we were guided by the following question: how do these politically contentious and competing desires for Hawai`i inform how we relate to this place? While there are many theories and concepts we could use to try and answer this question, the course drew upon those that articulate Settler Colonialism and Decolonization.

The course asked the students to take seriously the `olelo no`eau (wise saying): Ho`okāhi no lā o ka malihini. Translated literally, it means “a stranger only for a day.” Figuratively, it means “after the first day as a guest, one must help with the work” (Pukui 1983, 115). Our work in the class was informed by a practice of relationality in which we “begin trying to see the world as networked together, and to understand how various actions and decisions will impact the world around us” not just in Hawai`i, but throughout Oceania (Lowman & Barker 2015, 118). Drawing upon methods and tools of critical mapping and critical literacy, we set out to explore how the places that surround us are imbued with politics.

These POP Tours invite each of you to interact with The Politics of Place, learn something new, and then help with the work.
